What the audit found (the short version)

  1. The homepage already leans visitor-first — good instinct, we keep that. But the #1 next step for a newcomer (the visitor form) is buried in the footer, there's no Plan a Visit page (/visit is a 404), no map, and the hero's main button bounces people off-site to YouTube.
  2. The sermon archive is a genuine asset: 3,062 sermons with machine transcripts, already searchable (Algolia) and already living in Airtable — your notes' "sermon API" mostly exists. The wireframes build the study experience on top of it.
  3. Events, calendar, and missions simply don't exist on the current site. No events page, no calendar, and the word "missionary" appears on zero pages. The old native app was the only channel for letters — screens 5 and 6 bring both onto the web.
  4. ⚠️ One urgent technical finding also came out of the audit — documented privately in docs/research/site-audit.md on the prototype branch. Rick: read that one first.
  5. PWA verdict on your two questions: sermon downloads — yes. CarPlay — no app icon (that needs a native app), but steering-wheel/Now Playing control works, and the church's existing podcast feed gets sermons into Apple Podcasts & Spotify, which do have CarPlay apps. Full table on screen 8.

Design notes

  1. One promise, one button. The page has a single job: get a first-timer to Sunday. Research calls Plan-a-Visit the most important page on a church site. P1
  2. Kids content is the visitor's #1 anxiety — check-in, safety, what happens at each age. Pulls from the ministries the church already runs (Sunday School, Jr. Church, Kid's Club, VBS). P1
  3. Map, parking, arrival. Today the address exists only as footer text — no map on the entire site. P1
  4. Keep Breeze as the backend, lose the bounce. Same form the church already processes, embedded so the visitor never leaves a page that looks like Beacon. P1
  5. Virtual tour (your 7/11 note). Walking into an unfamiliar building is the scariest part of a first visit; a short walkthrough video defuses it. Start simple — one steady phone-shot video on the church's YouTube, embedded here. It can grow into a fancier tour later without changing the page. P1

Design notes

  1. Keep the transcript search — it's the archive's superpower. Full-text search over machine transcripts already works via Algolia; we keep the exact-match quotes trick and make it the default experience. P1
  2. "Ask a question" is the entry point for the agentic sermon search your team is already building — a visitor asks "what has Pastor Blalock taught on assurance?" and gets sermons + passages back. Slot it in as a tab so it can ship whenever it's ready. P2
  3. Series become first-class. Featured current series with a follow action; each series gets its own page collecting the whole sequence. P1
  4. Play, download, favorite — right on the card. Today the list page can't play anything; the ⬇ maps to PWA offline downloads and ☆ to favorites (device-local first, synced to accounts in P3). Play is now the biggest tap target on every card, per your note. P1–P2
  5. Fix the taxonomy: "Sunday Morning" isn't a series. The current Series filter mixes service times with real teaching series (and its counts are wrong — inflated beyond the archive size by duplicated lookup values in the data). Split Service vs Series, add a real date filter (year → month → day, from your notes — handy for "that sermon two Sundays ago"), and add your notes' Tags/Topics. P1
  6. The podcast feed is the CarPlay answer. The church already publishes to Spotify; adding Apple Podcasts + a clean RSS means sermons show up in real CarPlay apps without app-store risk. P1

Design notes

  1. Readable URLs and rich metadata. Today a sermon lives at /sermon/recA4tSieAbOsYcIU — an Airtable record ID. Slugged URLs + server-rendered pages fix sharing (right now a shared sermon link previews as a blank page) and let Google index 3,062 sermons. Tags from your notes become the dashed chips. P1
  2. One player, all formats. Today you get a YouTube embed or a bare audio tag, and the "Audio" button opens a raw S3 MP3 in a new tab. Here: one persistent player with speed, a real labeled download (which doubles as the PWA offline action), favorite, and share. P1
  3. The transcripts already exist — every sermon has a machine transcription in Airtable that's currently only used as hidden search fodder. Surfacing it synced to the audio is what turns a listen into a study session. P1
  4. How the AI answers work — the three questions from your notes. Where do answers come from? Only the sermon transcripts (this one's, or the archive's on screen 3) — never the open internet. Where do they take you? Every answer quotes the transcript and links the timestamp — one tap jumps the audio to the pastor explaining it himself. How do you verify they're good? That's what the citations are for: the receipt is always one tap away, summaries are clearly labeled AI-generated, and when the transcript doesn't cover a question the tool says "not covered in this sermon" instead of guessing. So yes — AI summarizes the pastor's explanation, but only from his own transcribed words, never its own theology. P2
  5. Related-by-passage is nearly free — scripture references are already structured data in Airtable, so "other sermons on Psalm 119" is a query, not a project. P1

Design notes

  1. List by default, month grid on desktop. Research: lists convert better on mobile, calendars create date urgency on desktop — offer both, one dataset. P1
  2. "Subscribe to calendar" answers your notes directly — members add Beacon's calendar to Google/Apple via an ICS feed and it stays current forever. P1
  3. Featured events with capacity. VBS keeps its Breeze registration (all the child-info and waiver handling stays there) — but the event page is Beacon-branded and shows remaining spots. P1
  4. Lightweight RSVP for simple events where full Breeze registration is overkill — basketball, fellowships. One mechanism per event: RSVP or register, never both. P1
  5. Services stay separate from events. Weekly services live as a fixed block (and on Visit/homepage); the calendar is for the special stuff. Prevents "Sunday Morning" clutter — the same mistake the sermon Series filter makes today. P1
  6. RSVP without accounts, reminders later. Name + contact is enough at launch; SMS/push reminders arrive in P2, and RSVPs link to accounts in P3. P1–P3
  7. The thin-calendar plan — Rick's flag: "we don't have that many events." Recurring ministry rhythms (basketball, choir, fellowships, prayer meeting) live in the list as auto-repeating entries, so the next seven days always show life even when only one special event exists. List is the default view; the month grid is secondary and only earns its place when there's enough to fill it. Jason maintains one small events table — recurring entries mean most weeks need zero editing. P1

Your questions, answered (researched July 2026, sources in docs/research/pwa-findings.md)

CapabilityPWA?The reality
App icon on the CarPlay screenNOCarPlay apps must be native with an Apple-granted entitlement — the very door that kept slamming. No web app can do this.
Control sermons from the carYESAudio from the installed PWA routes through CarPlay/Bluetooth like any phone audio; the MediaSession API puts title/artwork on the car's Now Playing screen with steering-wheel play/pause/skip. You just tap play on the phone first.
True CarPlay experienceVIA PODPublish the sermon RSS to Apple Podcasts (Spotify feed already exists) — those apps have real CarPlay browsing. Zero app-store risk for the church.
Download sermons offlineYESService worker + on-device storage. Since iOS 17, installed web apps get up to ~60% of the phone's disk, and home-screen apps are exempt from Safari's 7-day cleanup in normal use.
Push notificationsYESiOS 16.4+ (must be installed to home screen — we teach that in-app), Android fully. New letters, event reminders. SMS stays an option for the least-techy members.
App store removal riskGONEThere is no store, no review, no policy exposure. The church owns the whole channel.

Design notes

  1. The PWA is the website — one codebase, member-tabbed shell when installed. The five tabs map exactly to your notes: sermons, calendar, letters, give. P1
  2. Downloads are the native app's killer feature, kept. Explicit ⬇ per sermon, a manager showing storage used, and graceful re-download if the OS ever evicts. The car hint sits right in the player. P1–P2
  3. iOS has no automatic install prompt — Android does. So the app teaches Add-to-Home-Screen itself, because install unlocks both push and reliable storage on iPhone. P1
